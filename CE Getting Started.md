# Character Engine — Getting Started

The fastest way in isn't building a whole world. It's one character, made through a conversation.

## 1. Open the Wizard and start a Chat

Tap **Wizard** in the toolbar, then go to the **Chat** tab. This drops a live chat right into your story document — write a new paragraph and press Send (you don't need to type curly braces; the script handles that for you).

You *can* use the Wizard to build out a whole world — locations, factions, plot — but none of that is required to get moving. A single character is enough.

## 2. Develop the character in chat

Talk through who this character is the way you'd describe them to a friend: who they are, what they want, how they talk, what they look like, their history. Treat it as a conversation, not a form — ask the wizard questions, let it push back, follow tangents. The more the chat actually establishes about them, the better the character that comes out of it.

## 3. Generate the character — one at a time

The Wizard can generate characters in a batch from a chat's context. **Don't.** Generating one character at a time, from a chat that was actually about that one character, is far more reliable — the concept dump the generator works from is cleaner, and the result holds together better. Batch generation is there for volume, not quality.

## 4. Find them in the Characters tab

Once generated, the new character shows up in your **Characters** roster. Open them up — you'll land on four tabs: **Overview**, **Static Profile**, **Dynamic Profile**, **Visuals**.

## 5. The character profile — edit and regenerate

**Overview** is the quick-glance summary. **Static Profile** is where the actual written fields live — physical description, personality, voice, background, and so on.

Every field here is plain text you can edit by hand. If you'd rather regenerate one, each field has its own regenerate button, and you can give it a short hint to steer the result ("make the voice more clipped," "she's more guarded about this") instead of accepting whatever comes out first. Small, targeted regenerations like this are usually faster than rewriting by hand.

## 6. The Dynamic Profile — how they change over time

This tab is where the character stops being static text and starts updating on its own as the story runs:

- **Hooks** — things pulling at the character that build pressure toward a resolution.
- **Ephemeral Thoughts** — a short line of what they're thinking *right now*; this is what gets injected into the Author's Note.
- **Mid-range State** — slower-moving context (current social stance, etc.) that folds into their lorebook entry.
- **Primary Relationship** — the one relationship the engine tracks in depth: how it started, its current status, and where it's heading.
- **Texting Persona** — governs how they read if you use Character Chat with them later.

Each of the first four has its own on/off switch at the top of the tab. Turning one off freezes its current content rather than clearing it — nothing is lost, it just stops updating.

## That's enough to start

One character, built through a chat, generated on its own, then reviewed and tuned in Static and Dynamic Profile. Locations, other characters, and the rest of the Wizard's world-building tools can all come later, once you're comfortable with this loop.
